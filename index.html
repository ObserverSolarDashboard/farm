🏡 1-Acre Off-Grid Homestead

Permaculture Zone Layout — Concentric Design

1

Kitchen Garden — Herbs, Salad Greens, Tomatoes

Place immediately outside your back door — you'll visit this 3-5 times daily. Build 4-6 raised beds (4×8 ft each, 12" deep) from untreated cedar, cinder blocks, or galvanized stock tanks. Fill with 50/50 compost and native soil amended with perlite for drainage. This is where you grow lettuce, spinach, herbs (basil, cilantro, dill, parsley, thyme, rosemary, oregano, chives), cherry tomatoes, and radishes.

Intensive planting technique: Succession sow lettuce and radish every 14 days throughout the growing season. Interplant tall crops (tomatoes) with shade-tolerant greens underneath. Use every vertical surface — trellises for beans and cucumbers on the north side of beds so they don't shade other plants. Aim for 3 harvests per bed per season: early cool-season crop → summer warm-season → fall cool-season.

2

Chicken Coop & Run

Position within 50 ft of the kitchen for easy egg collection and scrap feeding. Build an 8×10 ft coop (houses 12 birds at 4 sq ft each). Use 2×4 framing, plywood sheathing, and metal roofing that drains into your rainwater collection system. Orient the coop door and main windows south for winter sun exposure.

Attached run: Minimum 120 sq ft with 1/2" hardware cloth on ALL openings (not chicken wire — raccoons reach through it). Bury hardware cloth 12" deep in an L-shape extending 12" outward to stop digging predators. Inside: 3-4 nesting boxes (12×12×12", 1 per 3-4 hens, positioned lower than roosts), roosts at 3 ft height using 2×4s with the 4" side facing up (flat surface covers toes in winter), and a pop door (12×14") for daily access to the run or pasture.

Deep litter method: Start with 6-8" of pine shavings on the floor. Add fresh shavings on top as it soils. The composting bedding generates heat in winter and only needs full cleanout 1-2x per year. By spring, you have excellent garden compost.

3

Rabbit Hutches

Place near the garden so manure goes directly to beds. Build elevated hutches 30" off the ground using 2×4 frames with 1×2" welded wire mesh floors and a solid resting board (12×18" plywood) in each cage so rabbits can rest off wire (prevents sore hocks). Under each hutch, position a collection tray or let manure fall directly onto a compost windrow or garden beds. Rabbit manure is "cold" — it goes straight onto beds without composting.

Starter setup: Build 4 cages minimum: 1 buck, 2 does, 1 grow-out for litters. Each cage 30×36" minimum with a corrugated metal roof and 4" overhang on all sides. Add a nesting box (18×10×10" wooden box with 4" front lip) for each doe — remove when not breeding.

4

Composting Station — 3-Bin Hot Compost System

Build three 4×4 ft bins side by side from pallets, cinder blocks, or wire mesh. Bin 1: Fresh inputs (kitchen scraps, garden waste, animal bedding, weeds without seed heads). Bin 2: Actively decomposing (turn weekly with a pitchfork). Bin 3: Finished compost ready for garden beds.

Hot composting recipe: Layer green material (nitrogen: kitchen scraps, fresh manure, grass clippings) with brown material (carbon: straw, dry leaves, cardboard, wood chips) at a 1:3 ratio by volume. Chop or shred large pieces for faster breakdown. A well-built pile reaches 140-160°F within 3-5 days, killing weed seeds and pathogens. Turn when temperature drops below 110°F. Finished in 4-8 weeks with regular turning.

5

Water Collection & Filtration Hub

1

Main Raised Bed Garden — Root Crops, Brassicas, Legumes

This is your calorie production engine. Build 12-16 raised beds (4×12 ft, 12" deep for root crops). Use companion planting: tomatoes with basil, beans with corn, carrots with onions. Rotate annually on a 4-year cycle: nightshades (Year 1) → legumes (Year 2) → brassicas (Year 3) → roots/alliums (Year 4). This prevents soil-borne disease buildup.

2

Potato Patch — Dedicated 1,000 sq ft

Potatoes are your #1 survival crop by calorie density per square foot. At 100 lbs per 100 ft of row, a 1,000 sq ft patch yields 400-800 lbs. Use the trench-and-hill method: dig 6" trenches 30" apart, place seed potatoes 12" apart with eyes facing up, cover with 4" soil. As plants grow, hill soil and straw around stems every 2-3 weeks until mounds are 12" high. Mulch heavily with straw (6-8") to suppress weeds and keep tubers cool (green potatoes are toxic).

💡 Save your own seed potatoes: select the best, disease-free tubers at harvest, store separately at 38-40°F over winter. Cut into pieces with 2-3 eyes each before spring planting. Never plant grocery store potatoes — they're often treated with sprout inhibitors.
3

Three Sisters Garden — Corn, Beans, Squash

Allocate 1,500-2,000 sq ft for this Native American companion planting system. Build mounds 4 ft apart, 18" across, 6" high. Plant 4 corn seeds per mound center. Once corn reaches 6" tall, add 4 pole bean seeds around the corn. When beans sprout, add 2 squash seeds at the mound edges. The corn provides a natural trellis for beans, beans fix nitrogen for the corn, and squash leaves shade the soil (suppressing weeds and retaining moisture).

Why this matters for survival: Corn + beans = complete protein (all essential amino acids). Squash provides calories, vitamins, and stores 3-6 months. This single system produces a nutritionally complete diet. Use Painted Mountain corn (85-day, cold-hardy) for grinding into cornmeal and making tortillas.

4

Berry Patch & Season Extension

Cold frames: Build from salvaged windows (check estate sales, Habitat ReStore) hinged onto raised bed frames. Back (north side) 6" taller than front for sun angle. Prop open above 50°F to prevent overheating. Start greens in February, extend harvest into December. Cost: $10-30 each if using salvaged windows. Build 4-6 for maximum season extension.

1

Dwarf Fruit Tree Orchard

Underplanting: Comfrey, crimson clover, and daffodils (pest deterrent) around each tree. Comfrey mines deep minerals and provides chop-and-drop mulch 4-5x per season. Trees take 3-5 years to produce meaningfully — PLANT THESE YEAR ONE. Every year you wait is a year of production you never get back.

2

Goat Paddock with Rotational Grazing

Divide 5,000-8,000 sq ft into 3-4 paddocks using cattle panels + T-posts (the ONLY fencing that reliably contains goats). Rotate goats every 5-7 days; each paddock rests 21+ days between uses (this breaks parasite life cycles — critical for goat health). Plant paddocks with orchard grass, clover, chicory, and plantain (natural deworming properties). Allow browse on willow, mulberry, and blackberry along fence lines.

Nigerian Dwarf goats need 200 sq ft per animal minimum in pasture. A 3-goat herd on 4 paddocks works well on half an acre. Supplement with alfalfa hay in winter when pasture is dormant. Budget $2-4/linear foot for cattle panel fencing — this is your biggest infrastructure expense for goats.

3

Perennial Food Forest Understory

Between and around fruit trees, establish a layered food forest modeled on natural forest ecosystems. Seven layers: Canopy (fruit trees), understory (elderberry, hazelnut, serviceberry), shrub layer (currants, gooseberry, honeyberry), herbaceous (comfrey, rhubarb, asparagus, walking onions), ground cover (strawberries, creeping thyme, white clover), root layer (sunchokes, horseradish), vine layer (grapes, hardy kiwi on trellises).

This system becomes increasingly productive in years 3-10 with minimal maintenance. Once established, it produces food with almost no annual input — no tilling, no planting, no fertilizing. The system self-fertilizes through leaf drop, nitrogen fixation (clover, comfrey), and nutrient cycling. This is your long-term food security backbone.

Wild Forage & Beneficial Insect Habitat

Leave a 10-15 ft strip along the property edge unmowed. Seed with wildflowers (yarrow, echinacea, bee balm, goldenrod) and allow native plants: dandelion (edible greens + roots), plantain (medicinal — wound care), clover (nitrogen fixer + bee forage), lamb's quarters (extremely nutritious "weed" — higher in vitamins than spinach).

This strip provides: Free forage for rabbits and chickens year-round, habitat for pollinators and predatory insects (ladybugs, lacewings, parasitic wasps that control garden pests naturally), and a visual buffer that makes your productive areas less visible from roads.

💡 OPSEC: A property that looks like "wild edges with nice landscaping" draws far less attention than one that screams "survival homestead." The perimeter buffer doubles as visual security. Consider appearances from road-facing sides especially.

1

Cold Frames — Add 4-6 Weeks Each End of Season

Build from old windows hinged onto a raised bed frame. Back (north) side 6" taller than front (south) to angle glass toward the sun. Prop open on days above 50°F to prevent overheating — plants cook faster than they freeze. Start lettuce, spinach, kale, and radish in mid-February inside cold frames. In fall, keep greens producing through December. On sub-zero nights, drape with an old blanket or straw bales around the outside for extra insulation. No electricity needed.

Build instructions: Use a 4×8 ft raised bed as the base. Attach a salvaged window (or polycarbonate panel) to the back edge with hinges. Cut side triangles from plywood to fill the height difference. Add a prop stick (notched 1×2) to hold the lid open at various heights. Total cost with salvaged window: $10-30. With new polycarbonate: $40-60.

2

Low Tunnels — Quick Row Protection

Bend 10-ft lengths of 1/2" PVC pipe into hoops over beds, spaced every 4 ft. Secure with EMT snap clamps or rebar stakes driven into the bed sides. Cover with 6-mil greenhouse plastic (for maximum warmth) or Agribon AG-19 row cover (for light frost protection while allowing rain through). Secure edges with sandbags, bricks, or clips.

When to use: In spring, cover transplanted tomatoes and peppers for 2-3 weeks after setting out to harden off and protect from late frost. In fall, cover cool-season crops to extend harvest 3-4 weeks past first frost. In summer, use shade cloth (30-50%) instead of plastic to protect lettuce from bolting in heat. Cost: under $30 per 12-ft bed. Build 6-10 for maximum flexibility.

3

Wall-O-Water — Cheat the Last Frost by 3 Weeks

Water-filled tepees that create a microclimate 10-15°F warmer than ambient. The water absorbs solar heat during the day and releases it overnight, protecting the plant inside even when outside temps drop into the 20s. Set around tomato and pepper transplants 2-3 weeks before last frost. Remove once nighttime temps are reliably above 50°F. Reusable for 3-5 seasons.

4

Deep Mulch — In-Ground Cold Storage

After first hard frost, pile 12-18" of straw mulch over root crops left in the ground: carrots, parsnips, beets, turnips, rutabaga. The mulch insulates soil, preventing deep freeze. Dig roots as needed throughout fall and into early winter. Mark rows with tall stakes so you can find them under snow or mulch.

💡 Parsnips actually improve in flavor after frost — the cold converts starches to sugars. Leave them in the ground as long as possible. They're one of the most underrated survival crops: calorie-dense, easy to grow, and they literally get better with cold storage.

1

Foundation & Frame

Build 8×10 ft on a raised foundation (concrete blocks or pressure-treated 4×4 skids) elevated 12-18" off ground. This prevents rot, deters rodents, and gives chickens shade/shelter underneath. Frame with 2×4 studs 16" on center. Sheath with plywood or OSB. Metal roofing slopes toward a gutter for rainwater collection. Use screws, not nails — you'll need to repair and modify over the years.

Materials list: ~20 2×4×8', 6 sheets plywood, 3 sheets metal roofing, 1 roll hardware cloth (1/2" × 36" × 50'), 50 ft gutter, hinges, screws, paint/stain. Total: $300-600 depending on lumber prices. Reduce cost by using reclaimed materials — check demolition sites, Craigslist free section, and Habitat ReStore.

2

Ventilation — The #1 Mistake New Chicken Keepers Make

More chickens die from poor ventilation than cold. Install vents at the roofline (NOT at bird level on roosts). Provide 1 sq ft of ventilation per 10 sq ft of floor space. Cover all openings with hardware cloth. Moisture and ammonia from droppings cause frostbite on combs and respiratory disease. The coop should smell fresh, never like ammonia.

Design: Install hinged vent panels along the eaves on opposite walls. This creates cross-ventilation above the birds without creating drafts at roost level. In winter, you may close one side but NEVER seal the coop completely. Cold, dry air is healthy. Cold, damp air kills.

💡 Ammonia test: if you can smell ammonia when you open the coop door, ventilation is inadequate. Fix it immediately. Kneel to bird level — that's where the concentration is worst.
3

Interior Setup — Roosts, Nesting, Floor

Roosts: 2×4 lumber with the 4" side up (flat surface lets birds cover their toes with their body in winter — prevents frostbite). Allow 8-10" per bird. Place all at the same height, 2.5-3 ft up. Higher than nesting boxes (chickens always roost at the highest available point — if boxes are higher, they'll sleep in them and soil the eggs).

Nesting boxes: 12×12×12" enclosed boxes, 1 per 3-4 hens. Mount at 18-24" off floor. Line with straw. Add a small curtain (burlap strip) over the opening — hens prefer darkness and privacy for laying. Place a ceramic dummy egg in each box initially to show them where to lay.

Floor: Deep litter method. Start with 6-8" of pine shavings. As it soils, add fresh shavings on top. Stir occasionally. The lower layers compost in place, generating warmth. Clean out fully 1-2x per year (spring and fall). The resulting material is excellent garden compost after 2-3 months additional curing.

Predator-Proofing — Non-Negotiable

1

Crops to Grow for Chickens

Sunflowers (Mammoth): Plant 200+ heads. Each yields 1-2 cups of seed. Dry on the stalk, thresh, store in buckets. High in fat and protein — excellent winter feed. Amaranth: Each plant produces 40,000-60,000 tiny seeds. Thresh and feed whole. Comfrey: Cut leaves, wilt for 24 hours, then feed. Up to 25% protein content. Cut 4-5x per season from each plant. Mangels/Fodder Beets: Grow in a dedicated patch. Huge roots store in root cellar. Chop and feed in winter — keeps hens entertained and fed. Squash culls: Feed overripe and damaged squash whole. Chickens eat seeds, flesh, and all.

Black Soldier Fly Larvae (BSF): The ultimate free protein source. Build a BSF composter from a 5-gallon bucket with angled ramps. Feed it kitchen scraps. Larvae self-harvest by climbing out into a collection bucket. 40%+ protein, 35% fat. Chickens go absolutely wild for them. Year-round production in a sheltered location. Search "BSF bin DIY" for build instructions — dozens of proven designs online.

💡 In winter when pasture is dormant, you'll need to provide 100% of feed from stored sources. Plan your sunflower, grain, and fodder beet storage accordingly. A flock of 12 hens needs ~3 lbs of feed per day. Over a 5-month winter (Nov-Mar), that's ~450 lbs of stored feed.
1

Keeping Chickens Through Winter Without Power

Warmth: A well-insulated coop with deep litter (12"+ of composting shavings) stays 20-30°F warmer than outside. 12 birds generate significant body heat. Do NOT seal the coop — ventilation matters more than warmth. Healthy cold-hardy breeds (Orpington, Australorp, Wyandotte, Plymouth Rock) handle Boise/Prescott/Reno winters without supplemental heat.

Water: Use two sets of waterers — swap a frozen one for a fresh warm one morning and evening. Black rubber feed pans (Fortex brand) absorb solar heat and flex to dump ice easily. For extended cold snaps, insulate waterers with bubble wrap or straw bales around the base.

Eggs: Production drops 40-60% in winter without supplemental light. To maintain production, add a solar-powered LED on a timer to provide 14 hours total light (supplement morning hours, not evening — abrupt darkness makes birds unable to find roosts). Alternatively, accept the natural cycle — hens that aren't forced to produce year-round live longer and lay for more years.

Frostbite prevention: Apply petroleum jelly to large combs and wattles before hard freezes. Better yet, choose breeds with small pea combs: Buckeyes, Wyandottes, Chanteclers, Ameraucanas. Frostbite is caused by moisture, not cold — if ventilation is good, frostbite is rare even at -10°F.

1

Build Elevated Hutches

Frame from 2×4 lumber. Floors: 1×2" welded wire mesh (NOT hardware cloth — too fine, hard to clean; NOT chicken wire — too flimsy). Elevate 30" on sturdy legs. Each cage: 30×36" minimum for a single adult. Wire floor allows droppings to fall through for easy collection. Critical: include a solid resting board (12×18" plywood, ceramic tile, or untreated wood) so rabbits can rest off wire — wire-only floors cause painful sore hocks, especially in heavy breeds.

Roofing: Corrugated metal with 4" overhang on all sides. Must be completely waterproof — wet rabbits get sick rapidly. Slope away from the front so you don't get dripped on during feeding.

Doors: 12×12" wire door on the front of each cage, secured with a J-clip or carabiner latch. Make it large enough to insert/remove the nesting box. All edges filed smooth — rabbits cut their feet on sharp wire.

Winter Cold Management

Rabbits handle cold far better than heat — their fur is exceptional insulation. Down to 0°F is manageable with a few precautions: provide extra straw bedding in the cage (they'll burrow into it), block wind on the north side with a tarp or plywood panel, ensure water doesn't freeze (swap bottles/crocks 2-3x daily with warm water). Pregnant/nursing does need extra protection — an enclosed wooden nesting box (18×10×10") stuffed with straw and the doe's own pulled fur keeps kits alive even in sub-zero temps. Check on newborn kits frequently in extreme cold.

1

Breeding Basics

ALWAYS bring the doe TO the buck's cage (never the reverse — does are territorial and may injure the buck). Breeding takes 10-30 seconds. The buck will fall off to the side, sometimes with a grunt — that's a successful mating. Allow 2-3 matings, then return the doe to her cage. Some breeders re-breed 8-12 hours later for higher conception rates.

Gestation: 28-32 days (average 31). On day 27, place a nesting box (18×10×10" wooden box with a 4" front lip) in the doe's cage. She'll pull fur from her chest and belly and build a nest — this is normal and expected. Kits are born hairless, blind, and deaf. Check the nest within 12 hours of birth — remove any dead kits and count the litter. Doe nurses 1-2x per day (usually at night, for only 5 minutes). Don't worry if you never see her nurse — check if kits have round, full bellies.

Timeline: Eyes open at 10-12 days. Start eating solid food (hay) at 3 weeks. Wean at 6-8 weeks. Separate by sex at 10 weeks (they can breed at 12 weeks). Butcher at 10-12 weeks (~5 lbs live weight).

2

Rotation Schedule for Maximum Production

With 2 does, stagger breeding by 2 weeks so you always have one litter growing out while the other doe is pregnant. Breed doe again 4-6 weeks after kindling (6 weeks rest is more sustainable for doe health long-term). This produces 8-10 litters per year between 2 does = 60-80 kits = 150-200 lbs of dressed meat per year from a space the size of a parking space.

Record keeping: Track: date bred, date kindled, litter size, number survived to weaning, growth rates. Cull does that consistently have small litters (under 6) or abandon kits. Retain the best-growing daughters as replacement does. Replace the buck every 18-24 months to prevent inbreeding — trade with another breeder rather than buying new.

1

Humane Harvesting at 10-12 Weeks

Process when fryers reach 5 lbs live weight. Equipment: Sharp knife, gambrel or hanging hooks, bucket, clean water, cooler with ice. Method: Cervical dislocation (broomstick method — quick, humane, no equipment needed) or .22 CB cap to the base of the skull. Hang by hind legs from gambrel, remove head, bleed out completely (2-3 minutes). Make a shallow cut around each hind leg below the hock, slit the skin on the inside of each leg to the tail. Peel the skin down like removing a sock — rabbit skin separates easily from the meat. Remove organs (save liver, kidneys, and heart — excellent eating). Rinse thoroughly with cold water. Rest in ice water for 24 hours before cooking or freezing.

A practiced person can process a rabbit start to finish in 10-15 minutes. It's the simplest livestock to process — far easier than poultry (no plucking or scalding required). Practice will build speed and confidence. Process multiple rabbits in a batch session for efficiency.

💡 Save ALL organ meat. Rabbit liver is one of the most nutrient-dense foods on earth — high in iron, B12, and vitamin A. Hearts and kidneys are excellent pan-fried in butter with herbs. Waste nothing.
2

Pelt Tanning — For Clothing & Barter

Scrape all fat and membrane from the hide immediately after skinning (while still warm is easiest). Tack the hide flat on a board, flesh side up, and scrape with a dull knife or fleshing tool until uniformly clean.

Brain tan (traditional): Every animal has enough brain to tan its own hide. Blend the brain with warm water to make a slurry. Work the slurry into the hide thoroughly on both sides. Roll up the hide, let it sit 24 hours. Stretch and work the hide as it dries (this is what makes it soft — if you let it dry without working, it becomes rawhide). Smoke over a cool fire for 2-4 hours to set the tan and make it water-resistant.

Simpler method — egg tan: Beat 2 egg yolks with a tablespoon of water. Work into the fleshed hide. Roll, rest 24 hours. Stretch while drying. Faster and less messy than brain tanning. Results are nearly identical.

Uses: Mitten linings, hat linings, baby blankets, vest lining, barter goods. 6-8 tanned pelts sewn together make a warm lap blanket. In a disrupted economy, warm soft fur items become extremely valuable trade goods.

1

Fencing — Your #1 Priority and Biggest Expense

Goats test every fence constantly. Acceptable options (ranked): Cattle panels (16 ft × 50" welded wire) secured to T-posts with wire or zip ties — strongest, most escape-proof, easy to install. Woven wire field fence (not welded wire — they push through welded joints) with a hot wire at nose height (30") as psychological backup. Board fence with wire backing. NEVER rely on: chicken wire, electric-only fence (they learn to rush through), or smooth wire.

Installation: T-posts every 8 ft with wooden corner/gate posts set 3 ft deep in concrete. Add a single strand of electric at nose height — a solar-powered fence charger ($50-100) provides the psychological backup. For a 4-paddock system on 6,000 sq ft, budget ~600 linear feet = $1,200-2,400 in fencing materials.

💡 Test: if you can push the fence panel aside by leaning on it with your body weight, a goat will breach it within 48 hours. Make it rigid first, then add the electric line as a backup.
2

Three-Sided Shelter

Goats HATE rain and wet conditions but handle cold well. Build a three-sided shelter (open to the south/southeast) — 8×10 ft for 3 goats. Frame from 2×4s, sheath with plywood, metal roofing. Deep bedding (straw or pine shavings, 6"+) provides warmth and comfort. Clean when bedding exceeds 12" or smells of ammonia.

Interior: Keyhole hay feeder (prevents waste — goats pull hay out and drop it; a keyhole design makes them eat from inside). Mineral feeder at nose height (away from rain). Separate area for buck if keeping on-site (bucks smell strongly and the odor can taint doe milk if housed too close). Elevate all feed and water off the ground — goats refuse anything that's been walked on, soiled, or contaminated.

3

DIY Milking Stand

Build from scrap lumber: a raised platform (18" high, 20×48") with a head stanchion at one end. The stanchion locks the goat's head gently while she eats grain — she stands still while you milk. Attach a small feed bucket at head height. Place the milking stand in a clean, covered area away from dust, flies, and other animals.

Milking procedure: Clean udder with warm water and a clean cloth. Milk into a stainless steel pail (not plastic — absorbs bacteria). Use a strip cup for the first few squirts from each teat to check for mastitis (lumps, discoloration = infection). Milk until udder is soft and empty. Filter immediately through a disposable milk filter or clean muslin into glass mason jars. Chill rapidly — cold water bath if no refrigerator. Raw goat milk keeps 5-7 days chilled. Without refrigeration: make cheese, yogurt, or kefir the same day.

1

Fresh Chèvre (Goat Cheese) — Simplest Recipe

Heat 1 gallon milk to 86°F (use a thermometer — accuracy matters). Sprinkle 1/4 tsp mesophilic culture over surface, let rehydrate 2 minutes, stir gently with an up-and-down motion (not circular — don't break curds). Add 2 drops liquid rennet diluted in 1/4 cup cool non-chlorinated water. Cover and let sit undisturbed 12-18 hours at room temperature (65-72°F). Curds will form a solid mass pulling away from the whey.

Ladle curds gently (don't break them) into butter muslin or fine-weave cheesecloth. Hang and drain for 6-12 hours (longer = firmer cheese). Salt to taste (1/2-1 tsp per cup of cheese). Mix in fresh herbs: chives, garlic, dill, cracked pepper, sun-dried tomatoes. Yield: ~1 lb cheese per gallon of milk. Keeps 1-2 weeks in a cool place. Stock up now: mesophilic culture and liquid rennet — these are the only inputs you can't produce on the farm.

2

Hard Cheese — Ages Without Refrigeration

Farmhouse cheddar: heat milk to 90°F, add mesophilic culture (1/4 tsp/gallon), ripen 45 min. Add rennet (1/4 tsp/gallon diluted in water), set 45 min until clean break. Cut curds into 1/2" cubes. Slowly raise temperature to 102°F over 30 min while gently stirring. Drain whey. Salt curds (2 tbsp/gallon). Pack into a cheese mold lined with cheesecloth. Press at 20 lbs for 1 hour, flip, press at 40 lbs for 12-24 hours.

Air-dry the wheel on a mat for 2-3 days, flipping twice daily, until a firm rind develops. Wax with cheese wax (melt wax, dip or brush onto wheel) and age in your root cellar at 50-55°F for 2-12 months. Properly waxed hard cheese stores 1-2+ years. A 2-doe operation producing 2-4 quarts/day can stockpile 50-100+ lbs of aged cheese annually — this is preserved milk that lasts through winter and beyond.

3

Goat Milk Soap — Premium Barter Item

Cold process: Freeze goat milk into cubes. Slowly add lye (sodium hydroxide) to the frozen milk cubes (prevents scorching — never add lye to liquid milk). Mix until dissolved. In a separate container, melt and combine oils: 30% olive oil, 30% coconut oil, 30% lard/tallow, 10% castor oil. When both lye-milk and oils reach 90-100°F, combine and blend with a stick blender until "trace" (consistency of thin pudding). Add essential oils if desired (lavender, peppermint, tea tree). Pour into silicone molds. Unmold after 24-48 hours. Cure on a rack with good airflow for 4-6 weeks (lye neutralizes during curing).

Barter value: In a disrupted economy, soap becomes extremely valuable within weeks. One gallon of milk makes 40-50 bars. Goat milk soap is gentle, moisturizing, and universally valued. Stock now: 50+ lbs lye ($1/lb), bulk coconut oil, olive oil, and essential oils. Lye stores indefinitely in sealed containers. This is your barter economy foundation.

1

Hoof Trimming — Every 6-8 Weeks

Buy quality hoof trimmers (Zenport Q91 or similar — $15-20). Secure the goat on the milking stand with grain for distraction. Look at the hoof from the bottom: the walls (hard outer edges) grow over the sole like overgrown fingernails. Trim overgrowth back flush with the sole — you're creating a flat, even walking surface. Trim in thin slices. If you see pink, stop — you've reached the quick. Apply blood stop powder if you nick it (keep a bottle on hand). Overgrown hooves cause lameness, joint stress, and foot rot.

Learning: Watch YouTube tutorials from reputable sources (Tennessee Meat Goats, Fias Co Farm). Practice on an experienced goat owner's animals before working on your own. It's not difficult but requires confidence — hesitant trimming leads to incomplete trimming which leads to problems.

2

Internal Parasites — #1 Health Threat

Barber pole worm (Haemonchus contortus) is the primary killer. It's a blood-sucking stomach worm that causes anemia and death. FAMACHA scoring: Pull down the lower eyelid and check the inner membrane color. Score 1-5: Score 1-2 (deep red/pink) = healthy. Score 3 (pink) = monitor. Score 4-5 (pale pink/white) = severe anemia, deworm immediately.

Prevention (best strategy): Rotational grazing — never let goats graze below 4" grass height. Parasite larvae live in the bottom 2" of grass; taller pasture = fewer larvae ingested. Rest each paddock 21+ days minimum. Plant chicory, birdsfoot trefoil, and sericea lespedeza — these have natural anthelmintic properties.

Treatment: Deworm ONLY when FAMACHA indicates need (targeted treatment prevents drug resistance — resistant parasites are the real crisis in goat health). Stock fenbendazole (Safeguard — dose at 2x cattle rate for goats: 10mg/kg) and ivermectin (oral, 0.4mg/kg — 2x cattle rate). Rotate drug classes annually. Herbal maintenance: garlic cloves, wormwood tea, black walnut hull tincture — useful for prevention, not for acute infections.

💧 Water Systems CRITICAL

1

Calculate Your Collection Potential

Formula: roof sq ft × rainfall inches × 0.623 = gallons collected/year.

2

Build the Collection System

Gutters: Half-round aluminum or vinyl on all collection roofs. Metal roofs are cleanest; avoid treated wood shingles (leach chemicals). First-flush diverter: Critical. The first 10-15 gallons from each rain event contain bird droppings, dust, and debris. Build from 3" PVC pipe (24-36" long) with a ball valve at the bottom — it fills with the dirty first water, then diverts clean rain to your tanks. Drain the diverter after each rain event.

Filtration: 100-micron mesh screen at each downspout entry to catch leaves and debris. Storage: IBC totes (275 gal, $50-75 used on Craigslist/Facebook Marketplace) or polyethylene cistern tanks. Elevate on a 4-6 ft platform for gravity pressure (~1.7 PSI per 4 ft of elevation). Paint totes or wrap in UV-resistant tarp to prevent algae growth. Overflow: Connect overflow pipes to a French drain or swale directing excess toward garden beds or food forest area.

Drinking water: Gravity filter (Berkey, Alexapure, or DIY ceramic filter) for potable water from rain or surface sources. Filters remove bacteria, parasites, heavy metals. A good gravity filter handles 2-6 gallons/hour — enough for a family of 4.

Install Alongside Electric Pump — Dual-System Approach

1

Design & Installation

Elevate a 500+ gallon tank (IBC tote) 4-6 ft above your garden area. Gravity provides enough pressure for drip emitters and soaker hoses. Run 3/4" mainline polyethylene pipe from the tank, with 1/2" drip lines branching to each bed. Install a 150-mesh filter at the tank outlet to prevent emitter clogging. Add a mechanical timer (spring-wound, no batteries — Orbit makes these) for hands-free watering.

Drip irrigation uses 50-70% less water than sprinklers and delivers water directly to root zones, reducing disease (wet foliage = fungal problems). In an arid climate, efficient irrigation is the single biggest factor in garden success or failure.

Ollas (clay pot irrigation): Bury unglazed terracotta pots next to high-value plants. Fill with water. The porous clay slowly releases moisture at a rate determined by soil dryness — completely self-regulating. One olla per 4-6 sq ft. Excellent for tomatoes, peppers, squash. DIY: glue two terracotta pots together lip-to-lip with silicone, seal one drainage hole, leave the other as a fill port. Cost: $3-5 each DIY. A garden of 50 ollas requires only a daily walk with a watering can.

🏺 Food Preservation — No Electricity CRITICAL

1

Location & Construction

2

Storage Times & Methods

Potatoes: 4-6 months (cure 2 weeks in dark at 60°F first, then store at 38-42°F). Carrots: 4-6 months (trim tops to 1", layer in damp sand in a wooden box). Beets: 3-5 months (trim tops, pack in damp sand). Cabbage: 3-4 months (pull up entire plant with roots, hang upside down from a rafter). Onions: 4-8 months (cure 2 weeks on a screen in warm dry air, store in mesh bags with airflow). Garlic: 6-8 months (braid stems and hang). Winter squash: 3-6 months (cure 2 weeks at 80°F, then store at 50-55°F — slightly warmer than root crops). Apples: 2-4 months (store separately from vegetables — they emit ethylene gas that hastens spoilage). Turnips/Rutabaga: 4-6 months (wax-dip for maximum life, or pack in sand).

💡 A well-managed root cellar with a full season's harvest feeds a family of 4 from October through March — no electricity, no canning, no processing needed. Build this in Year 1. It's the simplest and most reliable food storage system in existence.
1

Essential Equipment

Pressure canner: All-American brand (no gasket model — lifetime tool, never needs replacement parts). 21.5-quart model processes 7 quart jars or 19 pint jars per batch. This is the single most important preservation tool you'll own. Water bath canner: Any large stockpot with a wire rack works. Fuel: Propane burner (Camp Chef Explorer) for outdoor canning — faster than a stove and doesn't heat up the house. Stock 100+ lbs propane in refillable tanks. Long-term: a wood cookstove works for both canning methods.

Jars: Stock 200+ jars minimum (mix of quarts and pints, wide-mouth preferred). Ball or Kerr brand. Jars are reusable for decades; lids are single-use (stock 500+ extra). Tattler reusable lids ($1.50/lid) are a long-term investment — reusable 20+ years with replaceable rubber rings. Worth every penny for grid-down sustainability.

2

What Gets Water Bath vs. Pressure Canned

Water bath (high-acid foods only): Tomatoes (add 2 tbsp lemon juice per quart for safe acidity), pickles, salsa, jams, jellies, fruit, applesauce, sauerkraut, fruit butter. Process at 212°F boiling water for the time specified in USDA guidelines.

Pressure canner (low-acid foods — NO EXCEPTIONS): Green beans, corn, carrots, potatoes, peas, meat (chicken, rabbit, beef), broth/stock, soups, stews, chili. MUST be pressure canned at 240°F+ to prevent botulism.

⚠️ SAFETY: Follow USDA or National Center for Home Food Preservation guidelines EXACTLY. Do not use old family recipes without verifying they meet current safety standards. Botulism toxin is invisible, tasteless, and potentially fatal. When in doubt about acidity, pressure can it. When in doubt about a seal, discard it.

💡 Target: 200-400 jars per season for a family of 4. During peak harvest (Aug-Sep), you'll process 20-30 jars per week. Organize canning parties with neighbors — shared labor, shared equipment, faster processing, and community building.
1

Lacto-Fermentation — The Oldest Preservation Method

Salt creates an environment where beneficial Lactobacillus bacteria thrive and harmful bacteria cannot survive. No cooking, no energy, no equipment beyond a jar and salt.

Sauerkraut: Shred 5 lbs cabbage. Mix with 3 tbsp canning salt (NOT iodized). Massage and squeeze for 5-10 minutes until very juicy. Pack tightly into a wide-mouth half-gallon jar or crock. Press down so liquid covers cabbage. Weight down with a glass weight, sealed plastic bag of brine, or small plate with a rock. Cover with cloth (keep flies out, let gas escape). Ferment at 60-75°F for 3-6 weeks, tasting weekly until desired sourness. Move to root cellar. Keeps 6-12+ months.

Pickles: Pack whole cucumbers vertically in quart jar. Add: 3-5 garlic cloves, 1 head fresh dill, 1 tsp whole peppercorns, 1 grape leaf or oak leaf (tannins keep pickles crunchy). Fill with brine (1.5-2 tbsp canning salt per quart of non-chlorinated water). Weight down, cover, ferment 3-7 days at room temp. Taste daily. Refrigerate or root-cellar when done. Keeps 6+ months.

Stock NOW: 100+ lbs canning/pickling salt (NOT iodized — iodine inhibits fermentation). Crocks: 3-5 gallon stoneware (Ohio Stoneware or German Gärtopf with water-seal lids). Alternatively, wide-mouth half-gallon mason jars with glass fermentation weights. Also stock 10+ gallons white vinegar (5% acidity) for quick pickles. Better yet: learn to make your own vinegar from apple cider (apple scraps + water + sugar + time = infinite vinegar via acetobacter).

2

Solar Dehydration & Smoking

Smokehouse: Build from cinder blocks (8×4 ft footprint, 6 ft tall) with a metal roof and an offset firebox connected by a 6 ft underground flue pipe (cools the smoke). Cold smoking (70-90°F): Cheese, fish, bacon — flavoring and partial preservation. Hot smoking (200-275°F): Fully cooks and preserves chicken, pork, fish, sausage. Use fruit wood from your orchard prunings (apple, cherry, pear) — never softwood (pine, cedar — toxic resin).

Build a Portable, Disconnectable System

Build: 2× 200W panels ($150-200 each), 1× 30A MPPT charge controller ($80-120), 1× 100Ah LiFePO4 battery ($300-400), 1× 2000W pure sine wave inverter ($150-200). Total: ~$900-1,100.

This does NOT power: Refrigerator (use root cellar), space heater (use wood stove/rocket mass heater), AC (design for passive cooling), electric stove (use wood cookstove), well pump (use hand pump). Think of this as a communications and lighting system, not a lifestyle replacement.

💡 Solar event awareness: If your concern includes a micronova or geomagnetic storm, large fixed solar installations with long wire runs could be damaged by induced currents. A small, portable system that you can disconnect and store inside a Faraday cage (metal trash can lined with cardboard, lid sealed with aluminum tape) is more resilient than a permanent roof-mounted array. Keep panels disconnected when not in use.
1

Rocket Mass Heater — 80-90% Less Wood Than Conventional Stove

Burns small-diameter wood (sticks, branches, small splits) in a super-efficient J-tube combustion chamber reaching 1,800°F+. Exhaust runs through a long thermal mass (cob bench, brick mass) before exiting the chimney — by that point, exhaust is barely warm. The thermal mass absorbs heat and radiates for 12-24 hours after fire goes out.

Components: Feed tube (6" opening for vertical stick feeding), burn tunnel (horizontal, firebrick-lined), heat riser (insulated vertical pipe — 6" pipe inside 18" barrel filled with perlite), barrel (55-gal drum inverted over riser for radiant heat), thermal mass ducting (6-8" stovepipe through 15-25 ft of cob bench), chimney (short, 4-6", exits horizontally through wall).

Resource: "The Rocket Mass Heater Builder's Guide" by Erica & Ernie Wisner is the definitive construction manual. Build on a non-combustible base (concrete pad or brick hearth). Code compliance varies by county — rural areas are typically lenient for owner-built structures.

2

Wood Cookstove — Dual Purpose Heating + Cooking

Cast iron wood cookstove (Vermont Bud, Elmira, or vintage models on Craigslist) provides both cooking surface and space heating. Includes an oven box for baking bread, roasting meat, and dehydrating. Optional water jacket attachment heats water for washing. Heats a 1,000-1,200 sq ft space while cooking meals. Stock 3-5 cords of seasoned hardwood per winter season.

🌍 Soil Building & Fertility CRITICAL

1

The Fertility Cycle — Self-Sustaining by Year 3

Your goal is a closed loop: garden waste → compost → soil → plants → harvest → kitchen scraps → compost. Animal integration accelerates this: chicken manure (composted with bedding, very high nitrogen), rabbit manure (apply directly to beds — no composting needed, balanced NPK), goat manure (compost with bedding, good all-purpose). Comfrey is your secret weapon — plant 20-30 plants. Cut 4-5x per season, use as mulch. Comfrey roots mine nutrients from 6+ ft deep and concentrate them in the leaves — free, perpetual fertilizer.

Cover cropping: Sow empty beds in fall with winter rye (fixes carbon, prevents erosion, suppresses weeds) and crimson clover (fixes nitrogen, attracts pollinators). Chop and drop or turn in by early spring, 2-3 weeks before planting. Austrian winter peas are another excellent nitrogen-fixer.

Chicken tractor rotation: After harvesting a garden bed, move a portable chicken tractor onto it for 3-5 days. Chickens eat pest larvae, scratch in manure, consume weed seeds, and clear crop residue. Move to next harvested bed. By spring, every bed has been naturally fertilized and pest-cycled.

Magnetic Excursion Awareness

With the weakening geomagnetic field and accelerating pole movement, magnetic navigation becomes progressively less reliable. Stock: USGS 7.5-minute topographic maps for your county and surrounding areas (print and laminate). Learn celestial navigation (Polaris, sun position, seasonal star patterns) and terrain association. Teach these skills to every family member. A compass may become unreliable during an excursion — but the stars, sun, and terrain don't change.

Also consider: geomagnetic disruption may affect pollinator navigation (bees, birds). Diversify pollination strategies — hand-pollinate high-value crops (squash, fruit trees), maintain diverse native pollinator habitat (wildflower strips), and don't depend entirely on honeybees alone.

🔴 Core Operating Principles

Water is life. Every system design must center water conservation. Mulch everything. Drip irrigate. Store aggressively. Your hand-pump well is your most critical single infrastructure investment.

Skills over stuff. A $10,000 seed vault is worthless without the knowledge to grow, save, and preserve. Start small, build competence, expand gradually. Every season teaches lessons money can't buy. Practice NOW — every skill is 10x harder under stress.

Community is force multiplication. No family survives alone long-term. Build relationships with neighbors, local farmers, and like-minded people today. Trade skills, share surplus, organize seed swaps and skill-shares. Your network is your most valuable asset.

Redundancy in everything. Two water sources. Multiple preservation methods. Diverse crops across all seasons. Multiple protein animals. Manual backups for every electric system. If any single point of failure can collapse your food production, fix it before it matters.
